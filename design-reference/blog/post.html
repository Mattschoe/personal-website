On keeping a plain-text life

Jun 1, 2026

A few years ago I made a small, slightly obsessive decision: anything I wanted to keep for more than a week would live in a plain text file. Notes, recipes, todo lists, the half-formed ideas that used to die in a dozen different apps. One folder, one format, mine forever.

It sounds austere. In practice it's been the opposite — freeing in a way I didn't expect. When everything is just text, the tools become interchangeable. I can edit a recipe on my phone, search it from the terminal, and back it up the same way I back up my code.

Why plain text wins

The pitch is simple: plain text outlives the software that reads it. The note I wrote in 2019 opens instantly today, with no migration, no export, no "this format is no longer supported." Try that with a five-year-old proprietary app.

The best file format is the one you'll still be able to open in twenty years.

There's a second, quieter benefit. Constraints breed clarity. When you can't reach for a fancy layout, you focus on the words. When you can't tag and colour-code endlessly, you actually write the thing down.

The setup, briefly

  • One folder, synced with the same tool I sync everything else.
  • Markdown for anything that might become a web page.
  • A handful of small scripts to search, link, and publish.

That's it. No grand system, no productivity philosophy to buy into. Just files, in a folder, that will still be there long after I've forgotten which app was fashionable this year.

Where it falls down

I won't pretend it's perfect. Images are awkward. Sharing with non-technical friends means exporting to something prettier. And there's a real temptation to spend more time tending the system than using it — the plain-text equivalent of reorganising your bookshelf instead of reading.

But on balance, three years in, I haven't looked back. The stuff I care about is readable, searchable, and mine. That turns out to be most of what I wanted.
